Overview

Does it matter how you pour your Coca-Cola? This experiment investigates whether the method of pouring and method of consumption independently — and jointly — affect perceived taste ratings. The hypothesis: presentation and delivery method influence sensory experience just as much as the drink itself.

Research on carbonated beverages suggests consumers evaluate drinks heavily on carbonation, foam stability, and bubble activity — not just flavor. The way a drink is poured affects how much carbonation is retained, which directly impacts perceived taste quality.

// Blocking
Participants
7 participants as blocks
6 combinations each
Randomized order per subject

Each participant tasted all six combinations in a randomized order, with water palate cleanses and 5-minute rests between trials. Participants scored each drink 1–10 on taste. Bonferroni correction was applied for three simultaneous tests: α adjusted to 0.01667.

Results — All Factors Significant

The linear model produced a near-zero p-value overall (R² = 0.95, F = 51.9). All three factors were tested individually via ANOVA model comparison:

The Shapiro-Wilk test on residuals returned p = 0.000008, violating the normality assumption. Permutation tests (randomizing score assignment) were run for each factor. All three permutation p-values also came back at approximately zero, confirming the original ANOVA conclusions were valid.

Key Findings
01
Drinking from the can scored highest. The can condition consistently produced the top taste ratings across participants. Pouring into a glass — especially directly into the center — led to greater carbonation loss and lower perceived quality.
02
The interaction between factors matters. A straw generally improved scores — except when poured into the center of a glass, where it produced the lowest mean score of all six conditions. Drinking method can't be evaluated in isolation from pour method.
03
Assumption violations required a methodological pivot. When normality was violated, the analysis pivoted to permutation-based inference rather than forcing invalid parametric conclusions — a demonstration of rigorous, assumption-aware statistical practice.
04
Sample size was underpowered. Power analysis showed that ~50 replicates would be needed for adequate power. With only 7 participants, this experiment should be interpreted as exploratory — the next step would be replication at scale.
