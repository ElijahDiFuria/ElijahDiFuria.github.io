Overview

How much does lifestyle actually affect academic performance? This project builds a multiple linear regression model to quantify the effect of six lifestyle and habit variables on student exam scores, testing three hypotheses about study time, mental health, and daily habits.

The dataset — 1,000 simulated-but-realistic student records from Kaggle — includes daily study hours, mental health rating, sleep, exercise, social media use, and Netflix time. All six predictors were found to be statistically significant with p < 2×10⁻¹⁶.

Key Findings
01
Study time dominates all other predictors. Each additional hour of daily study adds ~9.6 points to exam scores. A student studying 4 hours versus 2 hours daily would score roughly 20 points higher — the difference between a C+ and an A−.
02
Mental health is the third strongest predictor. A student with a mental health rating of 9/10 would score approximately 15 points higher than one with a 1/10 rating. Academic interventions need to address mental wellbeing alongside study skills.
03
Screen time has meaningful negative costs. Four hours of social media daily versus two hours is associated with a ~5-point lower score. Netflix shows a nearly identical penalty. These are not small effects — they compound across a semester.
04
All assumptions were validated. Residuals vs. fitted showed random scatter, Q-Q alignment was strong, Shapiro-Wilk confirmed normality, and Cook's distance identified no unduly influential points. The model is valid for inference.
